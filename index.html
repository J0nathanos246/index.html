Abstract:

Linacs and cyclotrons are the two particle accelerators researched for this article.
Linacs accelerate particles in a straight line and uses oppositely charged drift tubes
connected to a pulse waveform to accelerate particles. Cyclotrons use a magnet split
into two dees oppositely charged to deflect the charged particles to follow a circular
path. With both of these examples electric and magnetic fields are in use, the particles
are only accelerated when crossing the gap between dees or drift tubes. Synchrotron
radian is used to find the particles relativistic momentum and charge, it also tells us
that any charged particle moving through a charged electric or magnetic field would
experience a force. The Lorentz’s factor is used for these particles when moving at
relativistic speeds. Theres also limitations that we face when dealing with particle
accelerators, these are the size and strength of magnets required to keep the high
energy beams on course and the size of accelerators used.

Introduction:

Today in the world there are more than 35000 particle accelerators, all used in a wide varity
of different applications in physics, chemistry, medicine and structure analysis. The largest
particle accelerator (Large Hadron Collider) can be found in Geneva Switzerland, this can
create beams of energy of 7 Tev.

Types of particle accelerators:

Linear accelerator:

Linear particle accelerators (linac) use accelerator modules aslo known as drift tubes, that are
cylindrical hollow electrodes, coupled behind each other connected to a pulsed waveform
that generates an accelerating gap between the two oppositely charged electrodes. For
positively charged particles, these oppositely charged particles repel the particle from
positive electrode and attracts them towards the negative electrode over the gap between
them. The gap between modules increases when particles have low energy this is due to the
particles accelerating at a greater magnitude when at low speeds, but when particles have
high energy the distance of the acceleration gap doesn’t change size. Reference3

Cyclotron:

Cyclotrons are a circular accelerator that uses a magnetic field to accelerate particles over a
gap. This gap is created by the magnet being split into two “Dees”, one of these dees is a
positively charged electrode and the other is a negatively charged electrode. The potential
difference between the electrodes creates an acceleration gap between them, a radio
frequency generator is used to excite the dees with an alternating field of constant
frequency. The use of a magnetic field means that the particles go over the same
acceleration gap multiple times increasing their velocity with every pass. With every pass the
accelerating particles trajectory radius increases as their speed increases, but their time
spent in the dees is constant. The maximum energy of the cyclotron is limited to the
diameter and the field strength of the magnet used.

Synchrotron radiation:

When an electron or a positron (a positively charged electron) is forced to move in a circular
path by a uniform magnetic field. In order to generate this circular path a force must be
exerted on to the charge to produce a constant acceleration across the whole radial path. If
the electrically charged particle moves through an electric or magnetic field the force acting
upon the particle is F = q(E + v x B). Where q is the charge of the particle, E is the strength of
the electric field, v is its velocity and B is the strength of the magnetic field. To see how the
momentum and charge of the particle changes as time goes on is shown through the
equations dP/dt = q(E + v x B) and dU/dt = ev x (E + v x b). The constant magnetic field always
produces a force on the charged particle that is at right angle to the particle's direction of
motion. The magnetic field is unable to change the energy of the charged particle. When the
strength of the electric field is zero these equations equate to dP/dt = ev x B and dU/dt = 0.
These charged particles can reach relativistic speeds, meaning P = ϒmv and U = ϒmc2.
Lorentzs’ factor ϒ is used when objects reach relativistic speeds, ϒ = (1 – (v2/c2))-1/2. The
relationship between the radius of the particles trajectory and its momentum can displayed
as P = BeR where R is the trajectory of the radius. For example, if we had an electron with an
energy of 2 GeV moving in a magnetic field with a known field strength of 1.2 T.
The trajectory of the radius of the moving electron would be 5.56 m. Reference2

Limitations:

There are many limitations when it comes down to accelerating particles. The size of the
required accelerator is one of the main issues, if we were to use a linear accelerator to
accelerate particles to the speed of light the size of the drift tubes used and the size of the
accelerator would be of a considerable size and using these for this application wouldn't be
feasible. The strength of the magnets used is another limitation, for example dipole magnets
of a known strength of 8.3 T would keep a beam of energy of 7 TeV on a path with a
curvature radius of 2.8 km. The strengths of the magnets used today are ever increasing in
size, magnets with a strength of 11 T have been used in some demonstrations and magnets
of a strength of 16 T have been envisioned to be used in the future. To overcome these
limitations physicists, use both electric and magnetic fields respectively to deflect charged
particle beams to reduce the amount of space required for the particle accelerator. The
stronger the respective fields are the greater the angle of deflection is, this allows particles to
be accelerated to the same energy but using a small area. Reference1. Reference4
